# Bot Management: Not All Automated Traffic Is Created Equal

Close to half of all internet traffic is generated by software, not people. Some of that is essential: Googlebot indexing your pages, Slack's link-unfurler previewing a URL someone shared, uptime monitors checking that your site is alive. Some of it is hostile: credential-stuffing scripts hammering your login form, scrapers stripping your content to train a model, price bots keeping a competitor updated on your catalog in real time. And some of it occupies a murky middle ground — AI crawlers that may or may not be honoring your robots.txt, depending on the company and the day.

Bot management is the practice of sorting this traffic, allowing the good actors through, and doing something useful about the rest. Vercel builds this into the platform as a set of configurable tools inside the Vercel Firewall, with two distinct managed rulesets for the two most common scenarios.

## How It Works

Vercel's bot management sits within the WAF layer of the Vercel Firewall, after DDoS mitigation and IP blocking but evaluated for every request that makes it that far. It operates through three mechanisms: a **bot protection managed ruleset**, an **AI bots managed ruleset**, and the ability to write custom WAF rules that reference bot-related signals.

The **bot protection managed ruleset** targets traffic that claims to be a browser but behaves like automation. It looks for clients that violate browser-like behavior — the wrong sequence of HTTP requests, missing cache headers, navigation patterns that no human would produce — and serves them a **JavaScript challenge**. The challenge requires the client to execute JavaScript and return a proof-of-work result. Real browsers handle this invisibly in milliseconds. `curl`, Python's `requests` library, and most scraping frameworks can't pass it at all.

The ruleset also catches **impersonation**: a `curl` request that sets its User-Agent string to claim it's Chrome still fails behavioral checks that a genuine Chrome browser would pass. The detection is less about what the client says it is and more about how it acts.

**Verified bots** are automatically excluded. Vercel maintains a directory of known legitimate bots — Googlebot, Bing, and dozens of others — and verifies them using multiple methods: IP address ranges, reverse DNS lookups (a request claiming to be Googlebot should resolve back to `*.google.com`), and increasingly, **cryptographic verification** via Web Bot Authentication, a protocol that uses HTTP Message Signatures (RFC 9421) to prove a request's origin. If a bot is in the verified directory, it passes through without a challenge even when the ruleset is active.

The **AI bots managed ruleset** addresses a newer category of automated traffic. Crawlers operated by AI companies — for training data, search indexes, or user-generated fetch requests — have proliferated faster than website operators have figured out how to handle them. Vercel maintains a continuously updated list of known AI crawlers and lets you choose what to do with them: log their visits without interfering, or deny them entirely. When a new AI bot appears in the wild, Vercel adds it to the list and applies your existing configured action automatically — you don't need to update anything.

You can apply each ruleset in **log mode** or **action mode** (challenge for bot protection, deny for AI bots). Log mode is useful for auditing: you see exactly what bot traffic is hitting your site before committing to blocking it. Once you're confident in the signal, you switch to action mode.

For more surgical control, WAF custom rules can target specific User-Agent strings or bot signatures — useful for blocking a specific crawler from your verified-bots list that you've decided you don't want. And for trusted automation (your own monitoring, your CI pipeline hitting a staging endpoint), you can create **bypass rules** that let specific traffic skip the bot protection ruleset entirely.

One important constraint: bot protection doesn't function correctly when a **reverse proxy** like Cloudflare or another CDN sits in front of your Vercel deployment. The behavioral signals that detection relies on get obscured by the proxy, leading to both false positives (legitimate users challenged unnecessarily) and degraded detection accuracy. If you're using a proxy, bot protection isn't the right tool.

## In the Wild

- A media site enables the bot protection ruleset in log mode for two weeks to baseline how much of their traffic is non-browser, then switches to challenge mode after confirming the signal quality.
- A developer tools company uses the AI bots managed ruleset in deny mode to prevent AI companies from training on their proprietary documentation without permission.
- An e-commerce store uses custom WAF rules to block the specific User-Agent strings of competitors' price-monitoring services while leaving Googlebot untouched.
- A SaaS product creates a bypass rule so its own Datadog synthetic monitors aren't challenged by the bot protection ruleset.
- A content publisher uses log mode on the AI bots ruleset to track which AI companies are crawling their content — and how frequently — before deciding on a monetization or enforcement strategy.

## What It Doesn't Do

Bot management doesn't handle authentication-layer abuse. If a bot obtains valid credentials and uses them, it looks like a legitimate user to the firewall. Rate limiting and application-level anomaly detection are the right tools for that scenario.

The bot protection ruleset also can't reliably distinguish between two well-behaved bots — one from a legitimate SEO tool you trust and one from a scraper that's implemented a convincing browser headlessness. For very sophisticated adversaries running headless Chrome with realistic behavioral patterns, detection accuracy degrades.
